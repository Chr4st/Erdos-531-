\title{A local lemma refinement of the \\
Balogh--Eberhard--Narayanan--Treglown--Wagner \\
lower bound for Folkman's theorem}

\author{Christ Xu}
\address{Department of Mathematics, University of Michigan, 530 Church Street, Ann Arbor MI 48109, USA}
\email{christ.xu1980@gmail.com}

\date{\today}

\subjclass[2020]{Primary 05D10; Secondary 05D40}

\begin{abstract}
Folkman's theorem asserts that for each $k \in \N$, there exists a natural number $n = F(k)$ such that whenever the elements of $[n]$ are two-coloured, there exists a set $A \subset [n]$ of size $k$ with the property that all the sums of the form $\sum_{x \in B} x$, where $B$ is a nonempty subset of $A$, are contained in $[n]$ and have the same colour. Balogh, Eberhard, Narayanan, Treglown and Wagner~\cite{BENTW} proved that $F(k) \ge 2^{2^{k-1}/k}$ for all $k \in \N$. Their proof uses a random colouring based on the $2$-adic valuation, followed by a union bound over all $k$-sets. We observe that the same random colouring combined with the Lov\'{a}sz local lemma gives the stronger estimate
\[
F(k) \ge 2^{2^{k-1}/(k-1) - O(1)}.
\]
Equivalently, for some absolute constant $C > 0$ and all sufficiently large $k$,
\[
F(k) > 2^{(2^{k-1} - Ck)/(k-1)}.
\]
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{intro}

Schur's theorem, proved in 1916, is one of the central results of Ramsey theory and asserts that whenever the elements of $\N$ are finitely coloured, there exists a monochromatic set of the form $\{x, y, x+y\}$ for some $x, y \in \N$. About fifty years ago, a wide generalisation of Schur's theorem was obtained independently by Folkman, Rado and Sanders, and this generalisation is now commonly referred to as Folkman's theorem (see~\cite{graham}, for example). To state Folkman's theorem, it will be convenient to have some notation. For $n \in \N$, we write $[n]$ for the set $\{1, 2, \dots, n\}$, and for a finite set $A \subset \N$, let
\[
S(A) = \left\{ \sum_{x \in B} x : B \subset A \text{ and } B \ne \emptyset \right\}
\]
denote the set of all \emph{finite sums} of $A$. In this language, Folkman's theorem states that for all $k, r \in \N$, there exists a natural number $n = F(k,r)$ such that whenever the elements of $[n]$ are $r$-coloured, there exists a set $A \subset [n]$ of size $k$ such that $S(A)$ is a monochromatic subset of $[n]$; of course, it is easy to see that Folkman's theorem, in the case where $k = 2$, implies Schur's theorem.

In this note, we shall be concerned with lower bounds for the two-colour Folkman numbers, i.e., for the quantity $F(k) = F(k,2)$. All logarithms are to the base~$2$. In 1989, Erd\H{o}s and Spencer~\cite{erdosspencer} proved that
\begin{equation}\label{es}
F(k) \ge 2^{ck^2/\log k}
\end{equation}
for all $k \in \N$, where $c > 0$ is an absolute constant. This bound stood until the recent breakthrough of Balogh, Eberhard, Narayanan, Treglown and Wagner~\cite{BENTW}, who established the doubly exponential lower bound
\begin{equation}\label{bentw}
F(k) \ge 2^{2^{k-1}/k}
\end{equation}
for all $k \in \N$. Their proof introduces a clever random colouring based on the $2$-adic valuation in which colours alternate along geometric progressions of the form $q, 2q, 4q, \dots$ for odd $q$. They show that any $k$-set $A$ with $S(A)$ monochromatic must have all $2^k - 1$ subset sums distinct, and that the monochromatic event then has probability at most $2^{1-2^{k-1}}$. A union bound over at most $(en/k)^k$ candidate sets completes their argument.

Our contribution is the observation that the union bound in the argument of~\cite{BENTW} can be replaced by the Lov\'{a}sz local lemma. The reason this is possible is that the event $\{S(A) \text{ is monochromatic}\}$ depends only on the random bits indexed by the odd parts of the elements of $S(A)$, and a fixed odd integer can appear as the odd part of a subset sum for only polynomially many candidate sets. This yields an improved exponent.

\begin{theorem}\label{mainthm}
There is an absolute constant $C > 0$ such that, for all sufficiently large $k$,
\begin{equation}\label{nb}
F(k) > 2^{(2^{k-1} - Ck)/(k-1)}.
\end{equation}
Consequently,
\begin{equation}\label{nb2}
\log F(k) \ge \frac{2^{k-1}}{k-1} - O(1).
\end{equation}
\end{theorem}

The gain in the exponent over~\eqref{bentw} is
\[
\frac{2^{k-1}}{k-1} - \frac{2^{k-1}}{k} - O(1) = \frac{2^{k-1}}{k(k-1)} - O(1),
\]
so the lower bound improves by a multiplicative factor of $2^{2^{k-1}/(k(k-1)) - O(1)}$.

This short note is organised as follows. We recall the random colouring of~\cite{BENTW} and its key probabilistic estimate in Section~\ref{colouring}, give the proof of Theorem~\ref{mainthm} in Section~\ref{pf}, and conclude with some remarks in Section~\ref{conc}.

\section{The Balogh--Eberhard--Narayanan--Treglown--Wagner colouring}\label{colouring}

We recall the random colouring introduced in~\cite{BENTW}. Independently colour each odd integer $q \le N$ with a random bit $X_q \in \{0,1\}$. For each $n \in [N]$, write $n = 2^{v_2(n)} \odd(n)$, where $\odd(n)$ is odd, and define
\[
\chi(n) = X_{\odd(n)} + v_2(n) \pmod{2}.
\]
Thus the colours alternate along every geometric progression $q, 2q, 4q, 8q, \dots$ with $q$ odd.

We need the following estimate, which is the central probabilistic ingredient of~\cite{BENTW}.

\begin{claim}\label{probclaim}
For every $k$-set $A \subset [N]$ with $S(A) \subset [N]$,
\[
\mathbb{P}\big(S(A) \text{ is monochromatic}\big) \le 2^{1-2^{k-1}}.
\]
\end{claim}

\begin{proof}
We include the short argument for completeness.

If $|S(A)| \le 2^k - 2$, then two distinct nonempty subsets $B_1, B_2 \subseteq A$ have the same sum. Removing $B_1 \cap B_2$ from both, we may assume $B_1$ and $B_2$ are disjoint and still have equal positive sums. Let $x = \sum_{a \in B_1} a = \sum_{a \in B_2} a$. Then $x \in S(A)$ and $2x = \sum_{a \in B_1 \cup B_2} a \in S(A)$, but the colouring satisfies $\chi(2x) \ne \chi(x)$. Hence $S(A)$ cannot be monochromatic, and the probability is~$0$.

Now suppose $|S(A)| = 2^k - 1$, so all nonempty subset sums are distinct. For each odd $m \in \N$, define $G_m = \{m, 2m, 4m, \dots\} \cap [N]$; these progressions partition $[N]$. Let $s = \min_{a \in A} v_2(a)$ and write $a = 2^s a'$ for each $a \in A$. At least one of the integers $a'$ is odd. Among the $2^k$ subsets of $A$, exactly half yield
\[
\sum_{a \in B} a' \equiv 1 \pmod{2},
\]
and the empty subset is not among them. Hence there are exactly $2^{k-1}$ nonempty subsets $B \subseteq A$ for which $v_2\big(\sum_{a \in B} a\big) = s$. The corresponding $2^{k-1}$ sums are distinct and, having the same $2$-adic valuation, lie in distinct progressions $G_m$.

Define $B_A \subseteq S(A)$ by choosing exactly one element from each progression $G_m$ that meets $S(A)$. Then $|B_A| \ge 2^{k-1}$. The colours of the elements of $B_A$ are independent fair bits, since they depend on distinct random variables $X_m$. Therefore
\[
\mathbb{P}(B_A \text{ is monochromatic}) = 2^{1-|B_A|} \le 2^{1-2^{k-1}}.
\]
Since $S(A)$ monochromatic implies $B_A$ monochromatic, the result follows.
\end{proof}

\section{Proof of the main result}\label{pf}

In this section, we give the proof of Theorem~\ref{mainthm}. The key tool is the symmetric form of the Lov\'{a}sz local lemma (see~\cite{AlonSpencer}, for example).

\begin{lemma}[Symmetric Lov\'{a}sz local lemma]\label{lll}
Let $\{E_i\}_{i \in I}$ be a finite family of events. Suppose each event has probability at most $p$, and each event is mutually independent of all but at most $D$ other events. If $ep(D+1) \le 1$, then $\mathbb{P}\big(\bigcap_{i \in I} E_i^c\big) > 0$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Proof of Theorem~\ref{mainthm}]
Let $N$ be an integer to be chosen later, and use the random colouring described in Section~\ref{colouring}. Define
\[
\A = \left\{ A \subset \N : |A| = k, \ \sum_{a \in A} a \le N \right\}.
\]
It suffices to consider this family, since $S(A) \subset [N]$ implies $\sum_{a \in A} a \le N$. For each $A \in \A$, define the bad event
\[
E_A = \{S(A) \text{ is monochromatic}\}.
\]
By Claim~\ref{probclaim},
\begin{equation}\label{pbound}
\mathbb{P}(E_A) \le p := 2^{1-2^{k-1}}.
\end{equation}

For $A \in \A$, define $O(A) = \{\odd(s) : s \in S(A)\}$. The event $E_A$ depends only on the random variables $\{X_q : q \in O(A)\}$. Hence $E_A$ is mutually independent of every collection of events $\{E_B : O(A) \cap O(B) = \emptyset\}$. We define a dependency graph on $\A$ by joining $A$ and $B$ whenever $O(A) \cap O(B) \ne \emptyset$, and now bound its maximum degree.

Fix $A \in \A$. Since $|S(A)| \le 2^k - 1$, we have $|O(A)| \le 2^k - 1$. Fix an odd integer $q \in O(A)$. We count the number of $B \in \A$ such that $q \in O(B)$.

The condition $q \in O(B)$ means that there exists a nonempty subset $T \subseteq B$ and an integer $j \ge 0$ such that $\sum_{t \in T} t = q 2^j$. Put $M = q 2^j$; since $B \in \A$, we have $M \le N$. Suppose $|T| = r$. For fixed $M$ and $r$, the number of possible sets $T$ is at most $\binom{M-1}{r-1}$, and the remaining $k - r$ elements of $B$ contribute total sum at most $N - M$, giving at most $\binom{N-M}{k-r}$ possibilities (this bound is also valid when $r = k$). Therefore
\begin{equation}\label{countB}
\#\{B \in \A : q \in O(B)\} \le \sum_{r=1}^{k} \sum_{\substack{j \ge 0 \\ q 2^j \le N}} \binom{q 2^j - 1}{r-1} \binom{N - q 2^j}{k-r}.
\end{equation}

We estimate this sum. For $r = 1$,
\[
\sum_{\substack{j \ge 0 \\ q 2^j \le N}} \binom{N - q2^j}{k-1} \le (\log N + 1) N^{k-1}.
\]
For $r \ge 2$, we use $\binom{q 2^j - 1}{r-1} \le (q 2^j)^{r-1}$ and $\binom{N - q 2^j}{k-r} \le N^{k-r}$. The inner geometric sum satisfies
\[
\sum_{\substack{j \ge 0 \\ q 2^j \le N}} (q 2^j)^{r-1} \le 2 N^{r-1},
\]
so the $r$-th contribution is at most $2N^{k-1}$. Summing over $r = 2, \dots, k$ gives at most $2k N^{k-1}$. Combining with the $r = 1$ term, for $k \ge 1$ and $N \ge 2$,
\begin{equation}\label{perq}
\#\{B \in \A : q \in O(B)\} \le 3k(\log N + 1) N^{k-1}.
\end{equation}

Since $|O(A)| \le 2^k - 1$, the maximum degree in the dependency graph satisfies
\begin{equation}\label{Dbound}
D \le 3k \cdot 2^k (\log N + 1) N^{k-1}.
\end{equation}

Now choose
\begin{equation}\label{Nchoice}
N = \left\lfloor 2^{(2^{k-1} - Ck)/(k-1)} \right\rfloor
\end{equation}
where $C > 0$ is a sufficiently large absolute constant. Then $N^{k-1} \le 2^{2^{k-1} - Ck}$ and $\log N + 1 \le 2^k$ for all sufficiently large $k$. Hence
\[
D \le 3k \cdot 2^k \cdot 2^k \cdot 2^{2^{k-1} - Ck}.
\]
Since $3k \le 2^{2k}$ for all sufficiently large $k$, we obtain
\begin{equation}\label{Dfinal}
D \le 2^{2^{k-1} - (C-4)k}.
\end{equation}

Recalling~\eqref{pbound}, for sufficiently large $k$,
\[
ep(D+1) \le e \cdot 2^{1-2^{k-1}} \left(2^{2^{k-1} - (C-4)k} + 1\right) \le 2^{-(C-5)k}.
\]
Choosing $C$ sufficiently large, for example $C = 10$, gives $ep(D+1) < 1$. By Lemma~\ref{lll}, with positive probability none of the events $E_A$ occurs. Therefore there exists a two-colouring of $[N]$ such that no $k$-set $A$ has $S(A)$ monochromatic. Hence
\[
F(k) > N = \left\lfloor 2^{(2^{k-1} - Ck)/(k-1)} \right\rfloor,
\]
proving~\eqref{nb} and consequently~\eqref{nb2}.
\end{proof}

\section{Concluding remarks}\label{conc}

We conclude this note with a few remarks.

First, we emphasise that the argument uses exactly the same random colouring as Balogh--Eberhard--Narayanan--Treglown--Wagner~\cite{BENTW}. The improvement comes entirely from replacing the global union bound over all $k$-sets by a local lemma argument. This is possible because the event $\{S(A) \text{ is monochromatic}\}$ depends only on the random bits indexed by the odd parts of the elements of $S(A)$, and a fixed odd part can occur in the subset sums of only $O_k((\log N) N^{k-1})$ relevant $k$-sets.

Second, while our bound~\eqref{nb2} improves the exponent from $2^{k-1}/k$ to $2^{k-1}/(k-1) - O(1)$, a multiplicative gain of $2^{2^{k-1}/(k(k-1)) - O(1)}$, this lower bound remains considerably far from the best known upper bound for $F(k)$, which is of tower type; see~\cite{ub}, for instance. Closing this gap remains a major open problem.

Third, we note that the dependency bound~\eqref{Dbound} is unlikely to be tight, and a more careful analysis of the dependency structure may yield further modest improvements. However, any approach based on the colouring of~\cite{BENTW} is fundamentally limited by the probability estimate in Claim~\ref{probclaim}, and substantially improving the lower bound for $F(k)$ would likely require new colouring constructions.
